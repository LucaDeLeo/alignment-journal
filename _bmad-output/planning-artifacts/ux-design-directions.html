Quantifying Stability Of Non-Power-Seeking In Artificial Agents

Evan Ryan Gunter, Yevgeny Liokumovich, Victoria Krakovna
Submitted Jan 15, 2026 24 pages Agent Foundations
Abstract

We establish a formal framework for quantifying the stability of non-power-seeking behavior in artificial agents operating in Markov decision processes. Building on prior work characterizing power-seeking tendencies in optimal policies, we introduce a measure of "non-power-seeking stability" that captures how robust an agent's tendency to avoid power-seeking is under perturbations to its reward function, transition dynamics, and discount factor. We prove that for a broad class of environments, optimal policies exhibit a phase transition in power-seeking behavior as a function of the discount factor, and we characterize the critical threshold at which this transition occurs.

1. Introduction

A central concern in AI alignment is the tendency of sufficiently capable agents to seek power as an instrumental goal. Turner et al. (2021) formalized this intuition by proving that optimal policies in Markov decision processes tend to seek power, in the sense that they tend to keep options open and acquire resources. However, their results characterize the behavior of fully optimal agents, leaving open the question of how robust this tendency is.

In this paper, we address this gap by introducing a quantitative measure of non-power-seeking stability. Our framework allows researchers and practitioners to assess, for a given agent and environment, how much the agent's policy would need to change before it begins to exhibit power-seeking behavior. This is practically relevant because deployed agents are rarely fully optimal, and understanding the stability of their non-power-seeking behavior is crucial for safety assurance.

Our main contributions are threefold. First, we define a formal notion of non-power-seeking stability that is agnostic to the specific definition of power used. Second, we prove general bounds on this stability measure for common environment classes. Third, we identify a phase transition phenomenon: as the discount factor increases past a critical threshold, optimal policies abruptly shift from non-power-seeking to power-seeking behavior.
